About Me

I’m Kymberley Apiro — a London-based multimedia journalist and visual storyteller working across video, photography, and editorial research. My work sits at the intersection of breaking news, culture, and investigative curiosity. I’m interested in how power operates — through institutions, technology, policing, and media — and how those systems shape everyday life, particularly for marginalised communities. I’m drawn to stories that sit just beneath the surface: the ones that require patience, proximity, and trust. I’ve spent several years embedded within a major international news agency environment, gaining extensive experience across fast-turnaround breaking news, social-first video, and on-the-ground event coverage. This included commissioning and briefing contributors, shaping editorial angles, scripting and contextualising video stories, and working closely with photographers and videographers to deliver accurate, publishable content under pressure. Alongside desk-based editorial work, I’ve built a substantial body of self-directed reporting — producing and publishing hundreds of video clips and photo stories in my own time, often embedded at protests, cultural events, and moments of political tension. Visually, my practice is shaped by documentary traditions, photojournalism, and fine-art influences. I’m particularly interested in identity, environment, and belonging, and how those themes can be explored through self-portraiture, landscape, and archival material. I completed an MA in Photojournalism and Documentary Photography at UAL, where my work focused on hybridity, place, and the uneasy space between personal and national identity. I’m motivated by being close to history as it unfolds — bearing witness, asking better questions, and building stories that don’t disappear after the news cycle moves on. Whether working independently or in collaboration with editors, my aim is to produce journalism that is rigorous, visually considered, and grounded in care.
